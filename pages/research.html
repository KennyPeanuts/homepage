research.summary

My research focuses on the factors that control community structure and ecosystem function in lakes and streams. I have shown for example, that longitudinal changes in a dominant predator within a watershed limits crayfish diversity; highlighting the importance of strong species interactions in community assembly. My work on carbon cycling in Alaskan Arctic lakes addresses questions of functional controls and reveals unappreciated interactions between the effects and regulation of climate change on lakes. My results show that climate induced changes in lake transparency would limit microbially driven organic matter breakdown in lake sediments and result in greater carbon storage under future climate conditions.

These past research experiences have now led me to the intersection of these two avenues of inquiry, specifically, what is the interplay between community structure and ecosystem function? My future research is directed toward understanding how community structure affects and is affected by the cycling of materials in aquatic habitats, specifically small man-made ponds.

Due to human landscape modification, the number of small ponds is increasing and current evidence suggests that these systems will have important feedbacks into climate change and other forms of anthropogenic disturbance. Despite this, our understanding of the relationship between community assembly and material (e.g., carbon) cycling in these systems is incomplete. A better understanding of the factors that affect both the biodiversity and the movement of carbon though these systems will permit more sensitive predictions of the effects of increased human impacts on the biosphere.
